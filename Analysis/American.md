His struggle to engage with his nephew reflects the tension between the symbolic (family duties, social roles) and the real (deep personal loss).
    
3.  **WALL·E** – This film explores the Lacanian theme of "the Other," where the robots embody human projections of idealized identity and consumer culture. WALL·E’s relationship with Eve reflects Lacan's notion of love and desire—focusing on what is missing in the other and the search for completion through an external object.
    
4.  **The Florida Project** – The characters here, particularly Moonee, live on the margins of society, existing in the "Real," beyond the reach of traditional symbolic structures. The film highlights the tension between the "imaginary" (fantasy, childlike innocence) and the "symbolic" (adult responsibilities and societal expectations), showing how people cope with their unmet desires.
    
5.  **The Color Purple** – This film reflects themes of desire, trauma, and self-identification, with Celie’s journey encapsulating Lacan’s notion of the subject’s struggle for recognition and agency. Her development throughout the film can be seen as her attempt to reappropriate her desire, overcoming her oppression and gaining a sense of subjectivity and autonomy.
    
6.  **The Talented Mr. Ripley** – Ripley’s desire to become someone else is a central Lacanian theme, as he attempts to reconcile his ideal self with his perceived lack. His identity shifts and fragments, reflecting Lacan’s idea that the subject is always caught between the desire to be the other and the realization that such a desire leads to inevitable alienation.
    
7.  **Tár** – The character of Lydia Tár, a celebrated conductor, represents a complex relationship with power, image, and desire. Her internal struggles and eventual unraveling can be analyzed in terms of Lacanian theories of the gaze and the "big Other," reflecting how identity can be contingent on external validation and how the collapse of this validation leads to crisis.
    
8.  **Doubt** – The uncertainty and tension in this film align with Lacan’s theory of the "impossible knowledge." The characters are caught in the symbolic order, where truth is elusive and desire manifests through suspicion, rather than direct fulfillment, creating a constant state of tension and ambiguity.
    
9.  **The Hours** – The film deals with Lacanian themes of repetition and the search for meaning within an oppressive, symbolic order. The three women’s struggles across different time periods highlight the universal conflict between self-identity and societal roles, where each is constrained by the narratives imposed on them by the symbolic order.
    
10.  **A Ghost Story** – This film exemplifies Lacan’s idea of the "real" as something that is out of reach, represented by the ghostly figure trapped in time. The character’s inability to change the past and their struggle with absence and loss aligns with Lacan’s concept of the subject’s never-ending pursuit of a completeness that is forever unattainable.
    

In each of these films, the Lacanian themes of desire, identity, and the gap between the subject and the Other are powerfully at play, showing how characters navigate their roles within the symbolic structures of society and their personal attempts to reconcile the "Real" aspects of their lives.

# Lacanian interpretation of the options for a man who had their family killed by murder

In Lacanian psychoanalysis, the scenario of a man whose family has been murdered places the subject in radical confrontation with **the Real**—the unbearable, unassimilable trauma that shatters symbolic coordinates.

Here’s a breakdown of the **possible Lacanian positions** or options available to such a subject:

---

### **1\. The Master’s Revenge (Male Logic)**

-   **Acting out** in the name of symbolic reparation.
    
-   The man assumes the role of *Name-of-the-Father*, attempting to restore the symbolic order by delivering justice himself.
    
-   This fantasy of mastery shields him from castration by projecting the trauma onto an Other to be destroyed.
    
-   **Example:** *The Revenant*, *Gladiator*
    

**Lacanian Note**: This is a **neurotic defense**, where the subject constructs a narrative of revenge to cover the hole in the symbolic created by the loss.

---

### **2\. Collapse into Melancholia (The Real)**

-   The subject identifies with the loss, unable to symbolize it.
    
-   Unlike mourning (where the object is lost), in **melancholia**, the subject loses themselves *in* the object.
    
-   He becomes the loss, frozen in a relation to the impossible Thing (das Ding).
    

**Lacanian Note**: The **object a**, lost in the Real, becomes the haunting remainder that prevents speech, action, or desire from re-emerging.

---

### **3\. Psychotic Foreclosure**

-   The trauma exceeds the Symbolic’s capacity to contain it.
    
-   If the Name-of-the-Father is foreclosed (not inscribed in the unconscious), the murder can trigger a **psychotic break**, hallucinations, or delusional systems.
    

**Lacanian Note**: Without the anchoring function of the paternal metaphor, the Real invades, unmediated by fantasy.

---

### **4\. Sublimation (Artistic or Ethical Act)**

-   The subject redirects the unbearable jouissance into a socially legible form: art, ethics, activism.
    
-   Here, the trauma becomes **cause of desire**, not its foreclosure.
    

**Example**: Writing a book about grief, becoming a public voice for peace or justice.

**Lacanian Note**: This is the **sinthome**—a unique mode of knotting the Imaginary, Symbolic, and Real that sustains the subject.

---

### **5\. Displacement through Fantasy**

-   He creates an **imaginary frame** (a fantasy) to organize the trauma and shield himself from the Real.
    
-   This can include becoming a vigilante, joining a war, or projecting the loss onto a mission.

-   Frances and Sophie are **mirror doubles**. Frances identifies with Sophie as her ego-ideal, imagining a life “together” forever.

---

## 💔 **Objet petit a**

-   Sophie is the **objet petit a**: the elusive object that causes Frances’s desire.
    
-   Dance, New York, the artistic life, all also function as shifting forms of **a** — none fully satisfy.
    
-   Frances’s trajectory is not about obtaining, but **reconfiguring her relation to lack**.
    

---

## 🔄 **Fantasy and Its Traversal**

Frances’s fantasy: life as a perpetual creative adolescence with Sophie — no compromise, no aging, no disillusionment.  
But:

-   She’s forced to **traverse the fantasy** by failing: evicted, rejected, humiliated.
    
-   The **final dance piece** marks a moment where Frances **symbolizes her desire** in her own name (see below) — not Sophie’s, not anyone else’s.
    

---

## 🧮 **Topology: The Möbius Strip of Identity**

-   Frances shifts between positions: friend, artist, employee, daughter, lover — but **these positions invert on themselves**.
    
-   She is like a Möbius strip: **inside and outside blur** — she appears successful but is falling apart, appears lost but is finding herself.
    
-   The film's structure mirrors this: **episodic, recursive, fragmented** — no linear path, only loops of becoming.
    

---

## 🪪 **The Final Scene: Naming the Lack**

-   The film ends with Frances labeling her mailbox: **"Frances Ha"** — a cut-off surname, like a signifier that lacks the full Name-of-the-Father.

-   The cut name = **acknowledgment of lack**, but also a **new symbolic place**.

*Frances Ha* is not a coming-of-age story in the traditional sense. It is a **coming-to-lack**:

-   From **narcissistic imaginary cohesion** (Frances + Sophie)
    
-   To **symbolic incoherence and crisis**
    
-   To a tentative **subjective positioning through desire**, failure, and repetition
    

Frances doesn’t find the "answer." She finds her **relation to the question**.

# The Others 2001 Lacanian analysis

**Lacanian Analysis — *The Others* (2001)**  
*Dir. Alejandro Amenábar – A gothic psychological horror film centered on Grace (Nicole Kidman) and her two photosensitive children, living in a fog-shrouded house after World War II.*

---

## 🧠 Overview

*The Others* is a masterclass in the **Lacanian Real** breaking into the Symbolic. It stages a collapse of certainty, identity, and knowledge, leading to the ultimate **unveiling of the subject as dead** — a pure encounter with lack.

---

## 🧩 I. **RSI – Real, Symbolic, Imaginary**

| **Register** | **Manifestation in the Film** |
| --- | --- |
| **Imaginary** | Grace’s self-image: a good mother, faithful wife, rational believer. The **home as safe haven**. |
| **Symbolic** | Religious law, rules, naming of children, routines, moral code (esp. Catholicism). |
| **Real** | The return of the repressed: the children’s illness, the dead servants, ghosts, and finally her own murder of her children. The Real as death that cannot be assimilated. |

> The Real: **You are the ghost.**  
> This final reversal is a pure Lacanian moment: **the subject is where it thought it was not**, and **what it thought was external was internal**.

---

## 🪞 II. **The Mirror Stage & the Inverted Gaze**

Grace's identity is built on the fantasy of being **the guardian of the home** — an ego-image supported by religious belief and maternal sacrifice.

But as the film progresses:

-   The mirrors are covered.
    
-   Sight is denied (blindness, darkness, the children’s illness).
    
-   The gaze returns **from the Other** — the **séance scene**, where the living see her as the ghost, **collapses the subject’s fantasy**.
    

> This is a return of the **gaze as object a** — the moment the subject sees itself as **object in the Other’s field of vision**.

---

## 💀 III. **The Real of Death & the Foreclosure of Castration**

-   Grace has **foreclosed the Real of death**: she denies the possibility that she and her children are dead.
    
-   This foreclosure creates a **psychotic structure** in Lacanian terms — she does not symbolically register castration (loss, death), and so it returns as a **hallucinatory, haunted reality**.
    
-   The **fog** functions as a **topological screen**: separating the Real from the Symbolic, maintaining the illusion of life.
    

---

## 🧒 IV. The Children as Objet Petit a

-   The children are both **cause and object of her desire**:
    
    -   They anchor her identity (as mother),
        
    -   But also expose the trauma: she killed them.
        
-   They represent **the lost object**, both loved and abjected, drawing her into **a deadly repetition**.
    

> The children are *not* simply loved — they are the **site of unbearable jouissance** that had to be destroyed.

---

## ✝️ V. Religion and the Name-of-the-Father

-   Catholicism stands in for the **Symbolic Law** — the promise of salvation, order, and meaning.
    
-   Yet it **fails to contain the Real**:
    
    -   Grace’s guilt,
        
    -   Her act of violence,
        
    -   The ghostly, unresolved desire.
        
-   The **Name-of-the-Father** does not operate — it is only evoked to delay the collapse, but never truly cuts.
    

---

## 🔄 VI. Fantasy and Its Traversal

Grace lives within a **fantasy** of maternal righteousness, salvation, and life.  
The ending is a **Lacanian traversal** of this fantasy:

-   She confronts the truth: she is dead, she killed her children.
    
-   She finally accepts her place **as lack in the Other** — no longer the master of meaning, but its leftover.
    

This is not redemption — it’s **subjective destitution**.

---

## 🌀 VII. Topology: The House as Möbius Strip

-   The house operates as a **closed loop** — a Möbius space where **inside and outside fold into one another**.
    
-   Day and night, life and death, past and present **cannot be cleanly separated**.
    
-   The Real haunts every corner; the symbolic order no longer anchors meaning.
    

---

---

## ✨ Conclusion

*The Others* dramatizes a subject’s confrontation with the Real — not just with ghosts, but with her own structural **non-existence as a full subject**.  
It is a journey from **ego to object**, from **belief to destitution**, and from **fantasy to truth**.

# August: Osage county

**Lacanian Analysis – *August: Osage County* (2013)**  
*Dir. John Wells – Adapted from Tracy Letts’ Pulitzer-winning play, a tragicomic family drama full of bitterness, betrayal, and intergenerational trauma.*

---

## 🧠 Overview

*August: Osage County* is a **portrait of a psychically imploded family** structured around an absent patriarch (Beverly), a devouring matriarch (Violet), and daughters who orbit their parental voids. Lacanian analysis illuminates the family as a scene where the **Name-of-the-Father is foreclosed**, the **Law fails**, and **jouissance becomes toxic**.

---

## 🧩 I. **RSI – Real, Symbolic, Imaginary**

| **Register** | **Manifestation in the Film** |
| --- | --- |
| **Imaginary** | Narcissistic conflicts among the daughters; idealized versions of motherhood, family, femininity, love — all shattered. |
| **Symbolic** | Paternal authority (Beverly) is absent/suicidal; family rules and moral codes are broken. Incest, abandonment, and addiction operate unchecked. |
| **Real** | Death, incest, addiction, dementia, rage — elements that break the symbolic, that cannot be represented or resolved. The Real returns through the body (e.g. Violet’s cancer, drug use). |

---

## 🧬 II. **Sexuation and Maternal Jouissance**

-   **Violet (Meryl Streep)** is the incarnation of **maternal jouissance in its rawest, most obscene form**:
    
    -   She doesn’t desire, she **devours**.
        
    -   She doesn’t castrate, she **floods** the symbolic field with unregulated speech, accusation, pills, and cruelty.
        
-   Her daughters, especially **Barbara (Julia Roberts)**, are caught between:
    
    -   Identification with Violet (imaginary),
        
    -   Hatred and distance (symbolic rejection),
        
    -   The unassimilable truth of their **own failure to separate**.
        

> “This is the truth: you can't tell me what to do. You don’t get it — I’m running things now.”

---

## 🪞 III. The Family as a Closed Circuit of Jouissance

-   The family house is a **claustrophobic space** where:
    
    -   Jouissance circulates but **cannot be spoken**,
        
    -   Secrets (incest, adultery, suicide) are **known yet repressed**,
        
    -   Every effort to name the trauma (e.g., Barbara’s confrontations) leads to **collapse, not resolution**.
        

Like a **Klein bottle**, **inside and outside blur** — no escape.

---

## 🧒 IV. Name-of-the-Father: Foreclosed or Collapsed?

-   Beverly Weston, the father:
    
    -   Poet, drunk, absent, finally dead.
        
    -   His suicide **marks the foreclosure of paternal function** — he **abandons the symbolic**, leaving the field open to Violet’s unregulated jouissance.
        
-   No one steps into this gap.
    
    -   Barbara tries — fails.
        
    -   The men (Bill, Little Charles, Steve) are ineffectual, cowardly, or perverse.
        

Without the paternal metaphor, **the incestuous Real erupts**:

-   Little Charles is both cousin and half-brother,
    
-   Matriarchal control turns into psychological possession.
    

---

## 🪙 V. Objet petit a and Desire

-   Desire is **displaced and perverse**:
    
    -   Karen dates a pedophile.
        
    -   Ivy is in love with her half-brother (unknowingly).
        
    -   Violet is obsessed with **maintaining control over her daughters** as if they are objects.
        
-   Each character **misrecognizes the cause of their desire** — they chase shadows or engage in **defensive fantasy**.
    

---

## 🧠 VI. Superego and the Voice

-   Violet’s voice is **superegoic** in the Lacanian sense:
    
    -   Not just moralizing, but **demanding jouissance**.
        
    -   She wounds with words, her speech is an **invocation of the Real** — beyond the pleasure principle.
        

> “You’re the only woman I know who’s ever gotten everything she ever wanted.”  
> “That’s the problem. Life’s long.”

The voice here is **pure drive** — it doesn’t demand meaning, it **demands excess**.

## 🔚 Conclusion

*August: Osage County* is **not about healing** — it’s about **what happens when the symbolic disintegrates**, and **jouissance has no limit**.  
It dramatizes a family where no one can occupy the Name-of-the-Father, and the maternal voice saturates every crack. Each subject is left to **repeat**, collapse, or **flee** — but none find a truly symbolic solution.

Jasmine’s collapse is the collapse of the **Imaginary ego** built around wealth, prestige, and fantasy marriage. Her story dramatizes the **failure of symbolic support**, the return of **the Real of loss**, and the descent into **psychotic disintegration**.

## 🧩 I. RSI — Real, Symbolic, Imaginary

| **Register** | **Manifestation in *Blue Jasmine*** |
| --- | --- |
| **Imaginary** | Jasmine’s persona: wealthy, elegant, educated. Built on false identifications and appearances. |

> Her speech is **full of cracks**: repetitions, irrelevant tangents, unstable tenses.  
> The Real **erupts through language**, but cannot be symbolized.

---

## 🧠 II. Subject of the Enunciation vs. Subject of the Statement

Jasmine says:

> “I want to study interior design, I’m taking a course online, I just need a computer.”

She speaks from an **imaginary position** that **doesn’t align with her reality**. The **subject of the statement** (“I’m building a life”) doesn’t align with the **subject of enunciation** (who is split, broken, desperate).

Her speech functions like **foreclosure** — she **denies the loss**, so the **Real returns in delusional form**.

---

-   **Hal (her husband)** occupied the paternal position — the **phantasmatic Name-of-the-Father** who gave her identity, prestige, place.
    
-   His arrest and suicide = collapse of this symbolic function.

She **tries to reinsert herself** into the Symbolic (dating the diplomat, studying design), but **can’t sustain the fiction**.

---

## 💬 IV. The Voice and the Superego

-   Jasmine **talks constantly** — not to communicate, but to **fend off the Real**.
    
-   Her speech is **disconnected from context**: she monologues in the street, at parties, alone.
    
-   This is not neurotic overthinking — it’s **psychotic foreclosure**: the **voice is no longer integrated into the subject**.
    

> Her voice = **superegoic imperative**: "keep speaking or you’ll vanish."

---

## 🧬 V. Fantasy, Loss, and Psychosis

-   Her **fantasy of the good life** with Hal was her entire psychic structure.
    
-   After its collapse, she tries to **reconstruct it in fragments**, but:
    
    -   She **can't mourn** it (there is no symbolic loss, only collapse),
        
    -   She **can't desire**, because she was only the object of Hal’s phallic power.
        

Like Schreber in Lacan’s analysis, **her delusion is a structure** — a way to **patch up the hole** left by the Real.

---

## 🔄 VI. Objet petit a

-   **Jasmine herself was objet a** for Hal — a decorative wife, his fantasy accessory.
    
-   After his death, **she loses her status as object-cause of desire**, and becomes:
    
    -   **An object without function**, an ornament with no place.
        
    -   This emptiness structures her psychosis — she *was* desired, now she is *nothing*.
        

---

## 🎭 VII. Topological Reading

Jasmine moves from **New York to San Francisco**, **past to present**, **illusion to truth** — but these are **not separate spaces**. They twist and collapse like a **Möbius strip**:

-   **Past and present** invert.
    
-   **Outside (the real world)** and **inside (her head)** collapse.
    
-   She tries to inhabit a **fantasy space that no longer exists**.
    

---

*Blue Jasmine* is not just a social critique; it’s a **psychotic portrait of subjectivity after symbolic collapse**.

*Judy*, starring Renée Zellweger as Judy Garland, offers a poignant entry point for a Lacanian reading, especially through the lens of **lack**, **object a**, **symbolic debt**, and the **Real of the voice**. Let’s break it down via Lacan's registers (RSI), his theory of desire, and the function of the voice in the formation of the subject.

### 🔴 **The Real**

The **Real** in *Judy* is the **irreducible trauma of the body**—the body as a commodity, the abused body of a child star, the collapse of the symbolic support that should sustain the subject. Judy’s insomnia, substance abuse, and moments of psychotic breakdown point to a subject in confrontation with an unbearable Real:

-   Yet, this object causes suffering. Her voice gives her fame, but it also expropriates her, making her an object for others.
    
-   The **Real of the maternal Other**: The monstrous mother (Ethel Gumm), enforcing strict control over diet, sleep, and even thought, becomes a figure of the **invasive jouissance of the Other**.
    

---

### 🟡 **The Symbolic**

Judy is crushed by the **symbolic debt** of being "Judy Garland."

-   “Judy” is a **signifier imposed** upon Frances Ethel Gumm (her birth name), a name she must embody.
    
-   The Symbolic is the Hollywood machine: Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer, the press, the fans. She becomes a **spokesperson for a desire not her own**, a puppet of Other people's jouissance.
    
-   Her family life, contracts, and performances represent the **law** that structures, but also suffocates her.
    

---

### 🔵 **The Imaginary**

The Imaginary is the mask Judy must wear: glamour, elegance, eternal youth.

-   In her declining years, the mirror no longer reflects the image she was meant to maintain.
    
-   Her **fragile ego** is split: between the decaying real body and the pristine, untouchable icon projected by the Imaginary.
    
-   Her relationship with her children is where this Imaginary flickers: she wants to be a “good mother,” but **the ego-ideal imposed upon her fails**.
    

---

### 💔 **Sexuation and Feminine Jouissance**

Judy’s suffering can be read as that of a subject **feminized by the gaze**. Not simply because she is a woman, but because she is **made object** of desire (object a).

-   Her position aligns with **feminine sexuation**: she does not “all” belong to the phallic function. There is a **beyond**, a **surplus-jouissance** (jouissance féminine) which breaks the boundaries of language, often appearing as sadness, exhaustion, or a sublimated **voice**.
    
-   Her voice carries this excessive jouissance. It **affects** others beyond words, yet **destroys her** in return.
    

---

### 🧩 **Object a (Voice)**

Lacan says the **object voice** is a **detached object**, a remnant of the body, both seductive and terrifying.

-   Judy’s voice is **an organ extracted from her**, desired by others, never fully hers.
    
-   Her songs function as **demand**, not just of love, but also recognition, rescue, or simply existence.
    
-   Singing becomes an act of subjectivation and **alienation** simultaneously.
    

---

### 🗝️ **The Final Scene: “Over the Rainbow”**

This iconic moment is pure **Lacanian structure**:

-   The song refers to an impossible place ("somewhere"), a **lost Thing** (das Ding), unattainable.
    
-   The **audience demands** this jouissance again. Judy tries to give it, but it breaks her.
    
-   It becomes a **sacrifice**: she gives herself as object a to the crowd, just as she was made to as a child.
    

---

### Conclusion

*Judy* shows us the **tragic structure of the subject as divided**, forced to carry the burden of the Other’s desire. Judy Garland, as presented in the film, is an object turned subject turned **broken object again**. Her life revolves around a **lost object (her childhood, her voice, her innocence, her love)**—which she can neither retrieve nor mourn completely.  
She is the ultimate figure of **melancholy within capitalism**: endlessly singing, endlessly falling, but never quite disappearing.

"Room" apresenta uma estrutura psíquica particular em torno da **relação mãe-filho** dentro de uma situação extrema de cativeiro. A narrativa se divide claramente em dois tempos subjetivos: **antes da libertação ("Room") e depois ("World")**, permitindo-nos ler a travessia de um **fantasma** e a reorganização do campo do **desejo**, do **gozo**, e da **entrada no simbólico**.

---

-   **Imaginário (I):**  
    O quarto representa um **mundo fechado**, auto-suficiente, onde mãe e filho vivem numa simbiose. A criança, Jack, desconhece a existência de um mundo exterior. A mãe, Joy, é o Outro total, o suporte imaginário da realidade.
    
-   **Simbólico (S):**  
    O simbólico está **forcluído** no "Room" — não há Nome-do-Pai, não há lei, não há alteridade. Apenas a linguagem da mãe e os objetos do cotidiano (como a pia ou o armário) que adquirem status de sujeitos. Após a fuga, inicia-se uma **reinscrição no simbólico**, com o trauma do mundo, a linguagem social, os nomes próprios, a alteridade radical.
    
-   **Real (R):**  
    O **gozo do sequestrador** (Old Nick) é o real mortífero, fora da linguagem, que invade e estrutura silenciosamente o espaço do quarto. Também se manifesta no **trauma da liberdade**, quando o retorno ao mundo real implica uma perda do sentido imaginário.
    

---

### 🧿 **Gozo e Trauma**

-   O quarto é também uma metáfora do **gozo encapsulado**. Jack representa o objeto a que o sequestrador não tem acesso — filho de um gozo ilegítimo.
    
-   O **trauma** não está apenas no cativeiro, mas na **tentativa de reintegração simbólica** após o trauma: como significar o que ocorreu?
    
-   A mãe busca apagar o gozo real com um novo significante: a liberdade. Mas **o gozo não se apaga** — retorna na forma da depressão, do silêncio, da tentativa de suicídio.
    

---

### ♂♀ **Sexuação e Nome-do-Pai**

-   L’absence de médiation paternelle est manifeste : Michael n’a pas de repères pour nommer ou situer cette femme dans l’ordre social.

-   Le **rapport sexuel n’existe pas** : leur relation est fondée sur une asymétrie radicale. Il y a jouissance, mais elle est hors langage. Hanna jouit d’une place obscure, et Michael devient son lecteur, puis son juge.

# The blind side 2009 - critique White savior German

Hier ist eine **kritische Lacan-inspirierte Analyse** von *"The Blind Side" (2009)* auf **Deutsch**, mit Fokus auf das **White Savior-Narrativ**, das **Imaginäre**, **Symbolische** und **Reale** nach Lacan, sowie gesellschaftlich-politische Implikationen:

---

### 🎬 **The Blind Side (2009)**

**Thema:** Kritik des White Savior-Narrativs  
**Regie:** John Lee Hancock  
**Hauptfiguren:** Leigh Anne Tuohy (Sandra Bullock), Michael Oher (Quinton Aaron)

---

### 🧠 **Lacanianische Lesart**

#### 🧩 Das Imaginäre – „Rettung“ durch Sichtbarkeit

Leigh Anne projiziert ein Idealbild von sich selbst als **guter, starker, moralisch überlegener Mensch** auf Michael. Im **Spiegel des Anderen** sieht sie sich als Retterin, was ihr ein narzisstisches Befriedigungsmoment gibt. Das Imaginäre dient dabei der **Verdeckung eines Mangels**: Sie handelt aus einem Bedürfnis heraus, sich selbst als „ganz“ zu fühlen.

#### 🧾 Das Symbolische – Struktur der Rassenordnung

Michael ist innerhalb der symbolischen Ordnung ein **Subjekt des Mangels**: arm, Schwarz, ohne Sprache, ohne Vater – er ist *nicht* ins Netz der symbolischen Zugehörigkeit integriert. Die Tuohy-Familie bringt ihn scheinbar in „die Ordnung“ – aber diese Ordnung ist **weiß, christlich, reich, sportlich und republikanisch**. Er wird **inkorporiert**, aber nur unter Bedingungen.

Leigh Anne fungiert als **Agentin des Gesetzes des Anderen** – des weißen, bürgerlichen Gesetzes. Ihre „Rettung“ basiert auf der Voraussetzung, dass sie die Ordnung *repräsentiert*, und Michael sich dieser Ordnung *anpasst*.

#### 🔥 Das Reale – das, was nicht gesagt wird

Das Reale hier ist die **Rassenfrage**, die koloniale Gewalt, der systemische Ausschluss. All das wird im Film **verleugnet oder romantisiert**. Michael bleibt ein **Objekt a** – das Objekt, das das Begehren der weißen Frau organisiert, aber **nie Subjekt** mit eigenem Begehren oder Sprache wird.

---

### ⚖️ **White Savior Kritik – dekonstruiert**

| Ebene | Kritik |
| --- | --- |
| Narrativstruktur | Weiße Frau = Retterin / Schwarzer Mann = passives Opfer |
| Subjektposition | Michael wird nicht als komplexes Subjekt mit Geschichte gezeigt |
| Imaginärer Fetisch | Der Schwarze Körper als moralische Projektionsfläche der Weißen |
| Politische Ökonomie | Systemischer Rassismus bleibt unsichtbar, weiße Familie = Held:innen |
| Begehren | Leigh Anne „sichert“ ihr Begehren durch die Figur Michaels, als wäre er ihr **verlorenes Kind** (vgl. *Objet a*) |
| Sprache / Schweigen | Michael spricht wenig – seine Subjektivität bleibt weitgehend stumm |

---

### 🌀 **Topologische Figuren**

-   **Paradoxe Inklusion:** Michael wird eingeschlossen, **indem er ausgeschlossen bleibt** (Lacan: *Verwerfung*). Er ist nur „drinnen“, weil seine „Andersheit“ nützlich oder rührend ist.
    
-   **Borromäisches Knotenmodell:**
    
    -   Symbolisches = Familie, Football, Universität
        
    -   Imaginäres = Gefühle, Fürsorge, Heroismus
        
    -   Reales = Rassismus, strukturelle Gewalt, Ausschluss  
        → Das Reale bleibt **unknackbar**, **ungeknotet**, **ausgeblendet**.

# The eyes of Tammy Faye 2021

**Lacanian Analysis: *The Eyes of Tammy Faye* (2021)**

This film traces the rise, fall, and partial redemption of televangelist Tammy Faye Bakker. From a Lacanian perspective, it provides fertile ground to analyze how the *imaginary*, *symbolic*, and *real* interact in the construction of identity, faith, and desire.

---

### **RSI (Real, Symbolic, Imaginary)**

| Register | Lacanian Reading |
| --- | --- |
| **Imaginary** | Tammy’s image-heavy persona: her makeup, smile, “performative” love—this masks fragility and disavowal. |
| **Symbolic** | Evangelical discourse, marriage, the law, money—all part of the “Name-of-the-Father” structuring her world. |
| **Real** | The collapse of the Symbolic (scandal, fraud) confronts Tammy with the Real of castration and lack—what resists symbolization (i.e. her husband’s sexuality, financial ruin, spiritual betrayal). |

---

### **Sexuation**

Tammy Faye aligns with the **feminine position** (in Lacan’s formulae of sexuation), not defined by "all" within a logic, but rather open to the Other, excess, and inconsistency. Her relation to faith and love—*“God loves everyone”*—places her outside the phallic function. She advocates for gay rights within a hypermasculine Christian logic that excludes this very act.

-   Her excess (makeup, love, song, emotion) is not *just* Imaginary—it is **surplus-jouissance**, gozo extra-fálico, often disavowed or mocked, but potent.
    
-   Her husband's queerness (implied) creates a **hole in the Symbolic**, which she patches through performative maternal and theological care.
    

---

### **Desire, Jouissance, and the Voice**

Tammy’s *voice*—literal and metaphorical—becomes a point of **drive** (Trieb): a pulsional repetition beyond meaning. Her faith-infused songs both mask and express her pain, marking a **zone of jouissance**. She both speaks and is spoken by the Other.

---

### **Conclusion**

*The Eyes of Tammy Faye* shows a subject who never fully occupies the place prescribed by the Other—her femininity is excessive, her love transgressive, her belief naive. Yet, in the face of collapse, she insists on speaking, singing, and desiring. She is a tragicomic figure of feminine jouissance, endlessly *not-all*—a saint of the *imaginary redemption* that never fully redeems.
